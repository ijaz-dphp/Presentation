# Public Financial Management (PFM) Act
## Group Presentation Deck

**Topic:** Public Financial Management (PFM) Act  
**Recommended Group Size:** 3–5 students  
**Recommended Duration:** 12–15 minutes  
**Recommended Slides:** 10–15 slides  
**Prepared for:** Classroom/academic presentation  

---

## Slide 1: Title Slide
**Title:** Public Financial Management (PFM) Act  
**Subtitle:** Accountability, Transparency, and Responsible Use of Public Resources  
**Include on slide:**
- Group members’ names
- Course title
- Lecturer/instructor
- Submission/presentation date

**Speaker notes:**
- Introduce the group and topic.
- Explain that the presentation examines the background, objectives, major provisions, institutional roles, and the importance of the PFM Act in strengthening accountability and transparency in the public sector.

---

## Slide 2: Introduction
**Slide content:**
- Public financial management refers to how governments plan, collect, spend, control, and report public money.
- The PFM Act provides the legal framework for managing state finances.
- It seeks to ensure that public resources are used efficiently, lawfully, and in the public interest.

**Speaker notes:**
- Begin by defining public financial management.
- Explain that every government needs a system that regulates budgeting, expenditure, revenue collection, accounting, reporting, and oversight.
- State that the PFM Act is important because it turns these financial management principles into enforceable rules.

---

## Slide 3: Background of the PFM Act
**Slide content:**
- Introduced to improve management of public funds.
- Responds to challenges such as misuse of funds, weak controls, poor reporting, and corruption risks.
- Establishes rules for budgeting, spending, accounting, and oversight.
- Supports modernization of public finance systems.

**Speaker notes:**
- Explain that many governments adopted PFM laws to address inefficiency and weak accountability in the public sector.
- Mention that before strong PFM frameworks, public institutions often faced problems such as delayed audits, unapproved spending, weak record-keeping, and inadequate monitoring.
- Note that the Act was designed to create order, discipline, and transparency in public finance administration.

---

## Slide 4: Objectives of the PFM Act
**Slide content:**
- Promote fiscal discipline
- Ensure efficient allocation of public resources
- Strengthen accountability in the use of public money
- Improve transparency in budgeting and reporting
- Enhance internal controls and audit systems
- Prevent waste, fraud, and unauthorized expenditure

**Speaker notes:**
- Explain that the Act is not only about controlling money but also about ensuring value for money.
- Fiscal discipline means government institutions should spend within approved budgets.
- Transparency means financial decisions and reports should be open and understandable.
- Accountability means officials must explain how they used public resources and face consequences for misuse.

---

## Slide 5: Key Provisions of the PFM Act I
**Slide content:**
- Preparation of annual budgets
- Approval of budgets by the legislature/parliament
- Revenue collection and management rules
- Expenditure control measures
- Limits on unauthorized or excess spending

**Speaker notes:**
- Explain that one major provision is that public money must be planned through formal budgets.
- The executive prepares the budget, but the legislature reviews and approves it.
- Funds should only be used for authorized purposes.
- Revenue raised by the state must also be properly recorded and managed.
- The Act discourages overspending and unauthorized withdrawals from public funds.

---

## Slide 6: Key Provisions of the PFM Act II
**Slide content:**
- Accounting and financial reporting requirements
- Internal control mechanisms
- Internal and external audit oversight
- Rules for management of public assets and liabilities
- Sanctions for non-compliance or misuse of funds

**Speaker notes:**
- Explain that proper accounting records are essential for tracking how money is used.
- Internal controls reduce the risk of fraud and errors.
- Internal auditors monitor compliance within institutions, while external auditors independently assess government accounts.
- The Act may also regulate borrowing, public debt, and asset management.
- Penalties for non-compliance are important because rules without enforcement are often ineffective.

---

## Slide 7: Roles and Responsibilities Under the Act
**Slide content:**
- **Parliament/Legislature:** approves budgets and exercises oversight
- **Minister of Finance/Treasury:** leads fiscal policy and financial management
- **Accounting Officers:** ensure proper use of funds in ministries/agencies
- **Internal Auditors:** review compliance and controls
- **Auditor-General:** conducts independent audits
- **Public Accounts Committee:** examines audit findings and follow-up actions

**Speaker notes:**
- Explain that the PFM Act works through clearly assigned responsibilities.
- Parliament ensures public funds are authorized and monitored.
- The finance ministry coordinates the national budget and sets financial rules.
- Accounting officers are directly responsible for how money is spent within institutions.
- Auditors and legislative committees help detect weaknesses and improve accountability.

---

## Slide 8: Importance for Public Sector Accountability
**Slide content:**
- Requires officials to justify financial decisions
- Creates clear lines of responsibility
- Strengthens audit and oversight systems
- Helps detect misuse, fraud, and waste
- Promotes consequences for financial misconduct

**Speaker notes:**
- Accountability means public officers do not simply spend public money without explanation.
- The Act makes it easier to identify who approved spending, whether procedures were followed, and whether outcomes matched plans.
- This improves discipline in ministries, departments, and agencies.
- A strong accountability framework also improves service delivery because funds are more likely to reach intended programs.

---

## Slide 9: Importance for Transparency
**Slide content:**
- Improves openness in budgeting and expenditure reporting
- Makes financial information available to oversight institutions and citizens
- Builds public trust in government institutions
- Supports informed debate about public priorities
- Encourages responsible management of state resources

**Speaker notes:**
- Transparency means financial information should not be hidden.
- When budgets, audit reports, and expenditure records are available, citizens and oversight bodies can better assess government performance.
- This reduces secrecy and can discourage corruption.
- Transparent financial systems also encourage confidence among donors, investors, and the public.

---

## Slide 10: Critical Analysis
**Slide content:**
**Strengths:**
- Provides a clear legal framework
- Improves budget discipline
- Strengthens oversight and reporting
- Supports better governance

**Challenges:**
- Weak enforcement in some institutions
- Political interference
- Limited staff capacity and technical skills
- Delayed audits and poor follow-up on findings
- Possible gap between law and actual practice

**Speaker notes:**
- Explain that although the PFM Act is important, its success depends on implementation.
- In many countries, the legal framework is strong on paper but weaker in practice.
- Challenges may include lack of trained personnel, poor systems, and limited political commitment.
- Therefore, the Act should be evaluated not only by its content but also by its results.

---

## Slide 11: Real-World Examples / Case Studies
**Slide content:**
- **Case 1: Budget control improvement** – stronger spending rules reduce unauthorized expenditure.
- **Case 2: Audit findings** – external audits identify misuse of public funds and recommend corrective action.
- **Case 3: Transparent reporting** – publication of financial reports improves citizen confidence and oversight.

**Speaker notes:**
- Example 1: In many public systems, tighter budget controls have reduced spending outside approved allocations.
- Example 2: Auditor-General reports often reveal irregular procurement, unsupported payments, or delayed accountability, showing why the PFM framework matters.
- Example 3: Where governments publish budget and expenditure information, civil society and parliament can better monitor public spending.
- If required by your lecturer, replace these with examples from your own country.

---

## Slide 12: Conclusion and Recommendations
**Slide content:**
**Conclusion:**
- The PFM Act is essential for the lawful, efficient, and transparent management of public funds.
- It promotes accountability, fiscal discipline, and better governance.
- Its effectiveness depends on implementation, oversight, and enforcement.

**Recommendations:**
- Strengthen enforcement of PFM rules
- Build capacity of public officers and auditors
- Improve digital financial management systems
- Ensure timely audits and implementation of audit recommendations
- Increase public access to financial information

**Speaker notes:**
- Summarize the central idea that the PFM Act helps protect public resources.
- Emphasize that accountability and transparency are not automatic; they require institutions, leadership, and compliance.
- State that stronger enforcement, training, and technology can help the Act achieve its goals more effectively.
